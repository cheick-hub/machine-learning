# 1. Probability Fundamentals for Machine Learning

## 1. Introduction

Machine Learning works with data that is uncertain, incomplete, and sampled from an unknown process. Probability gives us the language to describe that uncertainty.

If the dataset is:

$$
D = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}
$$

we usually assume that each observation was generated by some unknown distribution $P_X$:

$$
x_i \sim P_X
$$

Read this as: each $x_i$ is one sample drawn from the data-generating distribution of the random variable $X$.

The model does not know $P_X$ directly. It only sees finite samples and tries to learn patterns that remain useful on new samples from the same process.

**ML intuition:** training data is not the full truth. It is evidence about an unknown distribution.

---

## 2. Random Variables

A **random variable** maps the outcome of a random experiment to a numerical value.

$$
X : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
$$

where:

- $\Omega$ is the sample space: all possible outcomes.
- $X$ is the numerical value attached to an outcome.
- The value of $X$ is unknown before we observe the outcome.

In Machine Learning, features, labels, predictions, noise, and latent variables can all be modeled as random variables.

**Simple example:** for an email classifier, $Y$ can be a random variable where $Y=1$ means spam and $Y=0$ means not spam.

**Engineer note:** treating labels as random variables lets us model $P(Y \mid X)$ instead of expecting one perfectly deterministic label for every input.

---

## 3. Probability Distributions

A **probability distribution** describes how likely the possible values of a random variable are.

For a discrete random variable, we use a probability mass function:

$$
P(X = x)
$$

For a continuous random variable, we use a probability density function:

$$
p(x), \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} p(x)\,dx = 1
$$

The integral equals $1$ because the total probability over all possible values must be $1$.

Common distributions in Machine Learning:

| Distribution | What It Models | Typical ML Usage |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bernoulli | One binary outcome | Binary labels, binary events |
| Categorical | One of several classes | Multi-class classification |
| Gaussian | Values around a mean with noise | Regression noise, embeddings, priors |
| Poisson | Counts of events | Clicks, arrivals, rare events |
| Uniform | Equal probability over a range | Random sampling, initialization |

**ML intuition:** a distribution tells us what kind of randomness we believe generated the data.

**Engineer note:** choosing a loss function often corresponds to choosing a distributional assumption. For example, squared error is connected to Gaussian noise.

---

## 4. Expectation

The **expectation** is the long-run average value of a random variable.

For a discrete random variable:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x\,P(X = x)
$$

For a continuous random variable:

$$
\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} x\,p(x)\,dx
$$

Read this as: multiply each possible value by how likely it is, then add or integrate over all values.

Expectation matters in ML because most learning objectives optimize an average quantity, not a single example.

For a model $f$ and loss function $\ell(f(X), Y)$, the expected risk is:

$$
R(f) = \mathbb{E}_{(X,Y)\sim P_{X,Y}}\left[\ell(f(X), Y)\right]
$$

The true distribution $P_{X,Y}$ is unknown, so we approximate this expectation using the training dataset.

**ML intuition:** the real goal is low average loss on future data, not only low loss on the training rows we already saw.

**Engineer note:** empirical training loss is a sample estimate of expected risk. Generalization is about how close that estimate is to the true expected risk.

---

## 5. Variance

The **variance** measures how spread out a random variable is around its mean.

If:

$$
\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]
$$

then:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}\left[(X - \mu)^2\right]
$$

An equivalent and often useful formula is:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \left(\mathbb{E}[X]\right)^2
$$

Low variance means values stay close to the mean. High variance means values can move far from the mean.

**Simple example:** two models can have the same average accuracy, but one may be much less stable across datasets or random seeds.

**Engineer note:** mini-batch gradients have variance because each batch gives a noisy estimate of the full-dataset gradient.

---

## 6. Covariance and Correlation

**Covariance** measures whether two random variables tend to move together.

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y) =
\mathbb{E}\left[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)\right]
$$

where $\mu_X = \mathbb{E}[X]$ and $\mu_Y = \mathbb{E}[Y]$.

Interpretation:

- Positive covariance: $X$ and $Y$ tend to increase together.
- Negative covariance: one tends to increase when the other decreases.
- Covariance near zero: there is little linear co-movement.

Covariance near zero does **not** always mean independence. It means there is no strong linear relationship. Nonlinear dependence may still exist.

Correlation is normalized covariance:

$$
\operatorname{Corr}(X, Y) =
\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}
$$

where $\sigma_X$ and $\sigma_Y$ are standard deviations.

Correlation is easier to compare because it is always between $-1$ and $1$.

**ML intuition:** covariance and correlation describe relationships between features.

**Engineer note:** PCA uses the covariance matrix to find directions where the data varies the most.

---

## 7. Conditional Probability

Conditional probability measures the probability of one event given that another event is already known.

$$
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}
$$

where $P(B) > 0$.

Read this as: among the cases where $B$ happened, how often does $A$ also happen?

In supervised learning, we often want to estimate:

$$
P(Y \mid X)
$$

That is, the probability of a label $Y$ given input features $X$.

**Simple example:** $P(\text{spam} \mid \text{email text})$ is the probability that an email is spam after observing its content.

**Engineer note:** probabilistic classifiers usually output an estimate of $P(Y = k \mid X = x)$ for each class $k$.

---

## 8. Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem lets us reverse a conditional probability.

$$
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)P(A)}{P(B)}
$$

where:

- $P(A)$ is the **prior**: what we believe about $A$ before seeing $B$.
- $P(B \mid A)$ is the **likelihood**: how compatible $B$ is with $A$.
- $P(B)$ is the **evidence**: how likely $B$ is overall.
- $P(A \mid B)$ is the **posterior**: what we believe about $A$ after seeing $B$.

Read this as: update the prior belief using observed evidence.

**ML intuition:** Bayes' theorem formalizes learning from evidence.

**Engineer note:** Naive Bayes uses Bayes' theorem plus a simplifying independence assumption between features.

---

## 9. Likelihood

Probability and likelihood use the same mathematical object but answer different questions.

Probability asks:

> Given fixed parameters, how likely is this data?

Likelihood asks:

> Given the observed data, which parameters make it most plausible?

For a dataset $D$ and parameters $\theta$, the likelihood is:

$$
L(\theta; D) = P(D \mid \theta)
$$

If the samples are independent and identically distributed, often written i.i.d., then:

$$
P(D \mid \theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid \theta)
$$

Maximum Likelihood Estimation chooses the parameters that maximize the likelihood:

$$
\hat{\theta} =
\arg\max_{\theta} P(D \mid \theta)
$$

In practice, we usually maximize the log-likelihood:

$$
\log P(D \mid \theta) =
\sum_{i=1}^{n}\log P(x_i \mid \theta)
$$

The log form turns products into sums, which is easier to optimize and more numerically stable.

**Simple example:** for logistic regression, MLE finds the weights that make the observed labels most probable under the model.

**Engineer note:** minimizing cross-entropy for classification is equivalent to maximizing the log-likelihood of the observed labels under a categorical or Bernoulli model.

---

## 10. Independence

Two random variables are **independent** if knowing one gives no information about the other.

For discrete random variables, independence means:

$$
P(X = x, Y = y) = P(X = x)P(Y = y)
$$

for every pair of values $x$ and $y$.

For events $A$ and $B$, independence means:

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)
$$

Independence assumptions are common because they simplify calculations, algorithms, and theoretical proofs.

**ML intuition:** i.i.d. training data means samples are assumed independent and drawn from the same distribution.

**Engineer note:** independence assumptions are useful approximations, but violations can matter. Time-series data, user-session data, and duplicated samples often break independence.

---

## 11. How These Pieces Fit Together in ML

The main flow is:

$$
\text{data distribution}
\rightarrow
\text{sampled dataset}
\rightarrow
\text{estimated model}
\rightarrow
\text{prediction under uncertainty}
$$

Probability explains this flow:

- Random variables represent uncertain quantities.
- Distributions describe how values are generated.
- Expectations define average objectives such as risk.
- Variance describes instability or spread.
- Conditional probability describes prediction from inputs.
- Bayes' theorem describes belief updates from evidence.
- Likelihood connects data to parameter estimation.
- Independence assumptions make learning problems mathematically tractable.

---

## 12. Key Takeaways

- Probability is the foundation for reasoning about uncertainty in ML.
- A dataset is treated as a finite sample from an unknown distribution.
- A random variable represents a quantity whose value is uncertain before observation.
- A probability distribution describes how likely different values are.
- Expectation is the theoretical average behavior we usually want to optimize.
- Variance measures spread around the mean and connects to stability.
- Covariance and correlation describe relationships between variables, but zero covariance does not always imply independence.
- Conditional probability is the basis of supervised prediction.
- Bayes' theorem formalizes how evidence updates belief.
- Likelihood is central to parameter estimation and many training losses.
- Independence assumptions simplify ML theory, but they should be checked against the data-generating process.
